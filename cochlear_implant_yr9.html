How Normal Hearing Works

The Three Parts of the Ear

In normal hearing, sound passes through three parts of the ear before reaching the brain. Each part plays a critical role — if any part fails, hearing is affected.

An audiologist checking hearing health

Sound signals are ultimately processed by the brain

1
Outer Ear — The visible part of the ear and ear canal. Captures sound vibrations and directs them inward to the middle ear.
2
Middle Ear — Contains the eardrum and three tiny bones: the malleus, incus, and stapes. Sound vibrations cause these bones to move.
3
Inner Ear (Cochlea) — The snail-shaped cochlea contains thousands of tiny hair cells. These bend when fluid moves and send chemical signals to the auditory nerve.
4
Auditory Nerve → Brain — The auditory nerve carries electrical signals to the brain, where they are interpreted as recognisable sounds.

⚠️ What Goes Wrong?

Hair cells in the cochlea can be damaged by ageing, heredity, disease, infection, or loud noise. Damaged hair cells cannot send signals — so the auditory nerve is never stimulated and the person cannot hear. This is called sensorineural hearing loss. A hearing aid can amplify sound but cannot fix damaged hair cells. That's where the cochlear implant comes in.

What Is a Cochlear Implant?

Modern cochlear implants combine miniaturised electronics with precise surgical techniques

The Two Main Parts

A cochlear implant has two main components that must work together. One is implanted surgically inside the skull; the other is worn externally like a hearing aid.

Internal (inside skull)
📡 Receiver-stimulator — controls electrical pulses
📶 Antenna — receives signals from processor
🧲 Magnet — holds external part in place
🧵 Electrode wire — inserted into cochlea to stimulate nerve
External (worn outside)
🎙️ Microphone — picks up sound
💻 Sound processor — converts to digital signal
📡 Transmitter — sends signal to implant
🧲 Magnet — aligns with internal magnet

How the Cochlear Implant Works

Step by Step — From Sound to Brain

The cochlear implant follows a precise sequence of steps to convert sound in the environment into an electrical signal the brain can interpret. Each step must work correctly for the person to hear.

1
Microphone picks up sound from the environment around the person.
2
Sound is sent to the speech processor, a minicomputer worn externally.
3
Processor converts sound into a digital electrical signal — determining how much current will be sent to the electrodes.
4
Transmitter sends the signal wirelessly to the internal implant package via the aligned magnets.
5
Implant decodes the signal and determines how much electric current to send to each electrode. More current = louder sound. Electrode position = pitch.
6
Electrodes stimulate the auditory nerve directly — bypassing the damaged hair cells entirely.
7
Auditory nerve sends signals to the brain, which interprets them as recognisable sounds.

🧠 Fascinating Fact

Cochlear implants don't restore natural hearing — the sound quality is different and takes months of therapy to learn. Most infants, even those who have never heard before, can develop speech and language after receiving an implant. The brain is remarkably adaptable.
