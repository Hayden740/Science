🌡️ What is Global Warming?

Global warming refers to the long-term rise in Earth's average surface temperature, caused primarily by the burning of fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas. When these fuels are burned, they release greenhouse gases — mainly carbon dioxide (CO₂) and methane (CH₄) — into the atmosphere. These gases trap heat from the Sun that would otherwise escape into space, creating what is known as the enhanced greenhouse effect.

While the greenhouse effect is a natural process that keeps Earth warm enough for life, human activities have intensified it dramatically. Since the Industrial Revolution in the late 1700s, CO₂ levels in the atmosphere have risen from around 280 parts per million (ppm) to over 420 ppm today — the highest level in at least 800,000 years.

💡 The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) warns that if warming exceeds 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels, the effects on ecosystems and human societies will be severe and increasingly irreversible.

🌊 Rising Sea Levels

One of the most significant effects of global warming is the rise in global sea levels. This happens for two main reasons: thermal expansion (water expands as it warms) and melting ice from glaciers and ice sheets in Greenland and Antarctica.

Global sea levels have risen by approximately 20 centimetres since 1900, and the rate is accelerating. Scientists project that by 2100, sea levels could rise by a further 0.3 to 1 metre, threatening low-lying coastal regions. Island nations like Tuvalu and the Maldives face the real possibility of being almost entirely submerged. Coastal cities such as Jakarta, Miami, and Mumbai are already experiencing more frequent flooding.

⚠️ Approximately 1 billion people live in low-elevation coastal zones that are particularly vulnerable to sea-level rise and storm surges.

🔥 Extreme Weather Events

Warmer temperatures are making extreme weather events more frequent and more intense. Heatwaves are lasting longer and reaching higher temperatures — Australia experienced its hottest summer on record in 2019–20, with temperatures exceeding 49°C in some areas. Wildfires, fuelled by heat and drought, have burned millions of hectares across Australia, California, Greece, and Siberia in recent years.

Warmer oceans also provide more energy to tropical storms (hurricanes and typhoons), making them more powerful. At the same time, warmer air holds more moisture, which means when storms do occur, they can dump far more rainfall, increasing the risk of flash flooding. Paradoxically, some regions experience more severe droughts as evaporation increases and rainfall patterns shift.

🧊 Melting Ice & Permafrost

The Arctic is warming approximately four times faster than the global average. Arctic sea ice extent has decreased by around 40% since satellite records began in 1979. The Greenland ice sheet is losing mass at an accelerating rate, and glaciers around the world — from the Himalayas to the Alps to the Andes — are retreating at alarming speeds.

Beneath the Arctic tundra lies permafrost — permanently frozen ground containing vast amounts of organic material. As permafrost thaws, it releases stored CO₂ and methane, creating a dangerous feedback loop that further accelerates warming. This is one of several "tipping points" that scientists fear could trigger runaway climate change.

🔄 A feedback loop is when a warming effect causes further warming. Melting Arctic ice replaces white reflective surface with dark ocean, absorbing more heat — this is called the ice-albedo feedback.

🌿 Impacts on Ecosystems & Biodiversity

Global warming is disrupting ecosystems worldwide. Many species are shifting their ranges toward the poles or to higher altitudes seeking cooler conditions. The timing of natural events — such as flowering, migration, and breeding — is changing in ways that disrupt food chains. For example, some insect species are emerging earlier in spring, but the birds that feed on them may not have adjusted, leading to a mismatch.

Coral reefs are particularly vulnerable. When sea temperatures rise even slightly above normal, corals expel the algae living in their tissues, causing them to turn white — a process called coral bleaching. The Great Barrier Reef has experienced five mass bleaching events since 1998, with the most recent (2022) being the worst on record. Widespread coral death removes habitat for approximately 25% of all marine species.

👥 Impacts on People

The effects of global warming on humans are wide-ranging. Food security is threatened as droughts, floods, and shifting seasons disrupt agricultural production. The World Food Programme estimates climate change could push an additional 130 million people into extreme hunger by 2030. Water security is also affected — many communities depend on glacial meltwater for drinking and irrigation, but as glaciers retreat, these water sources are disappearing.

Climate migration is an emerging crisis. People are being forced to leave their homes due to floods, droughts, and rising seas. The World Bank estimates that by 2050, climate change could create up to 216 million climate migrants within developing countries. Rising temperatures also expand the range of vector-borne diseases like malaria and dengue fever, threatening public health systems.

Question 5 · Short Answer

What is permafrost and why is its thawing considered especially dangerous for the future of global warming? Use the term "feedback loop" in your answer.

0/800 characters

Model Answer Permafrost is permanently frozen ground found beneath the Arctic tundra, which contains enormous amounts of ancient organic matter. As global temperatures rise, the permafrost begins to thaw, releasing the greenhouse gases CO₂ and methane that were locked inside it. This creates a dangerous positive feedback loop: the warming releases gases that cause further warming, which causes more permafrost to melt, which releases more gases, and so on. This cycle could accelerate climate change far beyond current projections, making it one of the most feared "tipping points" in climate science — a threshold beyond which warming becomes very difficult or impossible to stop.
